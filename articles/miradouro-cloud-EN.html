What this doesn't say

The open question

Azure, AWS, GCP dominate the infrastructure. Where does the dependency get built? In the skills declared on LinkedIn, or earlier, in the architectures chosen by default in projects?

04

A question from the field

I'm actively sourcing cloud profiles right now. And over the past few weeks, I've seen salaries I wasn't expecting: architects with 15 years of experience at around €100,000 gross annually, excluding bonuses. Not exceptions. Market trends visible across multiple job postings, multiple countries.

This observation raised a meta-question: if senior cloud salaries are reaching these levels, what does that say about the structure of the cloud job market in Portugal? The rest of this article stays in that focus: Portugal, its available skills, its job listings, its salary levels. The European map in the sections above gave the context. We're now zooming in on the ground.

I looked for data. Here's what I found, and what I still don't know.

05

The visible market: job listings in Portugal

LinkedIn measures declared skills. itjobs.pt measures actual recruiter demand. Two different angles on the same phenomenon, and they tell a consistent story.

In April 2026, itjobs.pt, Portugal's main IT job board, shows 197 active listings mentioning Azure, 28 mentioning AWS, and zero mentioning OVHcloud. The ratio is 7:1 in favor of Azure. That's the state of the market on a day in April 2026.

The most solid hypothesis to explain this ratio: the composition of Portuguese nearshore. The Capgemini, Devoteam, Sopra Steria, Meritis hubs recruit for French and Belgian client projects, largely standardized on the Microsoft ecosystem. This is not a Portuguese technical choice. It's an inheritance from French-speaking nearshore demand.

"The cloud we recruit for is the cloud we'll master in 10 years. And the cloud we master is the cloud we'll propose to clients. Dependency isn't built in contracts. It's built in skills."

🧭
Field note · Jonathan

On a cloud architect position I sourced recently, the majority of profiles with 15+ years of experience were positioned above €100,000 gross annually, excluding bonuses. This is not aggregated data. But it was consistent across all the profiles I contacted.

One important methodological note: Hays data reflects actual placements by the firm, not statistical models. That's a strength. But Hays is positioned in the mid-to-senior market. Junior salaries may be underrepresented in their grid.

Methodological note: LinkedIn skills are self-declared, not verified certifications. LinkedIn penetration varies by country (~80% in the Netherlands, ~50% in Portugal), raw comparisons have structural limits. itjobs.pt covers the visible market only: internal hires and unpublished roles are not captured. OVHcloud's absence from job listings does not mean absence from actual usage. Hays salary ranges do not reflect variable bonuses or Lisbon/Porto/remote differentials.

07

What this opens up

The data gives a snapshot. What it doesn't give is an explanation. That's where it gets interesting.

Open questions

  1. Why does Azure dominate so much in Portugal? Is it really the French-speaking nearshore structure, or is there something more structural in the architecture choices of large IT services firms in Southern Europe?
  2. How did AWS become Amazon's golden goose? An infrastructure built to sell books that now generates the majority of the group's profits. It might be the greatest technology pivot story of the past 20 years.
  3. What is the future of infrastructure with AI? If models consume 10× more compute than traditional apps, do current hyperscalers remain the right operators, or are we seeing a new specialized infrastructure layer emerge?
  4. Portuguese Cloud Architects at 100k+: do they stay in Lisbon in 5 years, or does the combination of remote work and European salary convergence push them elsewhere?
