Cloud hiring.
But who, where, and at what price?

After a previous article on European digital sovereignty, I wanted to look at cloud through a more concrete lens: the people who build it. From Lisbon, the subject is getting harder to ignore. AWS still dominates declared skills across Europe. The UK concentrates a talent mass far above most of the continent. Portugal, meanwhile, looks much more Azure-driven in enterprise demand. And for the most senior profiles, salaries sometimes start to detach from the classic Portuguese economy.

01 Europe through skills

AWS is still ahead. But this is not only a market share story.

I had always heard the same hierarchy: AWS first, Azure second, GCP behind. LinkedIn Recruiter data confirms the order. But the interesting point is not only the ranking. It is what it says about the way hyperscalers also manufacture careers.

Hyperscalers do not only sell cloud. They build career ecosystems. Certifications, training credits, technical communities, documentation, developer events: all of this creates a form of professional lock-in. When an architect has spent several hundred hours on AWS or Azure, the skill also becomes a market identity.

AWS still has a very clear lead in declared cloud skills across Europe. Azure follows with a meaningful gap. GCP remains much smaller at continental scale.

These figures do not represent official certifications or actual occupied roles. They mostly reflect professionals choosing to publicly display a skill. But even imperfect, this snapshot remains useful: it shows where hyperscalers appear most embedded in European career paths.

02 Why the UK dominates

The UK already looks like a mature cloud economy

On the European map, the UK stands clearly apart. This is probably not a statistical accident. It is the combined effect of the City, fintech, public-sector cloud adoption and much earlier hyperscaler investment.

The UK is the real outlier. The City was one of Europe’s first major markets to industrialize cloud for data, distributed infrastructure and financial services. The public sector also played a role through G-Cloud. AWS announced £8 billion of UK investment for 2024-2028. The result is visible in the skills base: the country does not only appear to adopt cloud, it appears to have absorbed it into its labour economy.

03 Portugal focus

Portugal looks much more Azure-centric than the wider map suggests

Seen from Lisbon, Portugal does not look like a small isolated cloud market. It looks more like a European execution hub: enterprise-heavy, delivery-heavy, and much more exposed to the Microsoft ecosystem than a broad European reading would suggest.

Portugal is above 10K declared cloud skills. This remains small compared with the UK, Germany or France, but it is high relative to the size of the local market. That gap is exactly what makes the country interesting: part of the cloud demand operated from Lisbon is not Portuguese. It is European.

What this doesn't say

Azure appears particularly strong in Portuguese enterprise environments. EDP has publicly communicated a major migration to Azure. Other large Portuguese players, such as CTT and Millennium BCP, also appear regularly in the Microsoft ecosystem. Galp, however, is a better example of a multi-cloud reality: productivity and security on the Microsoft side, but also SAP workloads operated with AWS through IBM. So the conclusion is not 'Portugal is all Azure'. It is more precise: Microsoft seems very well placed in the corporate and transformation layer.

The open question

Nearshore probably amplifies this dynamic. Part of cloud hiring in Portugal serves French, Belgian or Luxembourg clients, often already strongly standardized around Microsoft. This is not definitive proof, but it is consistent with what can be observed in IT services firms and delivery hubs: Portugal does not only produce cloud for itself. It operates part of Europe’s cloud from Lisbon and Porto.

🧭
Field note · Jonathan

Across several recent cloud searches, profiles with more than 10 years of experience and real international architecture exposure could position themselves above €100,000 gross per year. This is not an aggregated statistic. It is a field signal. But this is exactly the kind of signal that often comes before official numbers.

This point matters: these levels do not describe the Portuguese economy as a whole. They describe a layer of the cloud market that already behaves like a European, sometimes global, market. An architect based in Lisbon may be benchmarked against French, British, Dutch or American needs. The local salary reference then becomes partial, not a natural ceiling.

This is also what makes Portugal hard to read. A company hiring a cloud engineer in Lisbon is not always hiring for the Portuguese market. It may be hiring for a French, Belgian or Luxembourg architecture, with technical and salary standards imported from elsewhere.

The cloud we recruit for today is probably the cloud we will offer tomorrow. Dependency is not built only in hyperscaler contracts. It is also built in available skills, architecture habits and the profiles the market knows how to produce.

Methodological note: LinkedIn skills are declared skills, not verified certifications. LinkedIn penetration varies by country, so raw comparisons have limits. Hays salary data gives a useful reading base, but does not always capture the rarest international profiles, freelancers or highly variable packages. The field observations mentioned here are qualitative and non-aggregated.

05

What this really says

The European cloud market is not homogeneous. The UK looks like a mature cloud economy. Germany and France have deep but more fragmented talent pools. Portugal seems to be building a specific position: smaller in size, but strongly connected to European demand, and increasingly strategic for Microsoft and large enterprise clients.

Open questions

  1. Can Portugal keep attracting senior cloud profiles if salaries progressively converge toward European standards?
  2. Is Azure simply more visible in Portugal, or is the country becoming a real Microsoft enterprise hub for Southern and French-speaking Europe?
  3. Is the UK a lasting exception, or will the finance + cloud + public-sector model spread elsewhere in Europe?
  4. When does European cloud dependency stop being an infrastructure topic and become a skills and salary topic?
